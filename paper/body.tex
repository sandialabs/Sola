\section{Introduction}

\begin{purpose}
High mathematical complexity leads to high implementation difficulty.
This paper is about lowering the difficulty of implementing a class of highly complex problems.
\end{purpose}

\noindent
Mathematics, algorithms, and implementation.
These three steps, sequential but intertwined, form the general approach to problems in computational science and engineering, statistics and data science, and scientific machine learning.
In the first step, a problem is formulated, models are proposed, and mathematical analyses lead to solution strategies; second, the components leading to a solution are grouped, ordered, and articulated as an algorithm; and finally, algorithms are implemented as executable software.
An implementation should then be verified --- checked for correctness --- before validating the overall approach against available test data~\cite{oden2004verification,oden2002estimation}.
Implementation difficulty increases rapidly as the complexity of the mathematical setting and the accompanying algorithms grows.
High-performance computing (HPC) seeks to address the computational challenges that arise from high complexity, but harnessing the power of HPC tools often requires expert-level knowledge and experience.
This paper presents a paradigm for developing algorithms and utilizing HPC tools in the highly complex setting of outer-loop problems.

\begin{purpose}
Outer-loop problems go beyond forward simulation. Introduce and motivate HDSA.
\end{purpose}

In contemporary computational science and engineering, an outer-loop problem is a procedure which requires many expensive ``forward'' simulations of a physical process to enable discovery, control, or decision making.
Examples include inverse problems~\cite{ghattas2021learning,stuart2010bayesianinverse}, optimal experimental design~\cite{alexanderian2021oed,aretz2024greedysensorselection,attia2018oed}, sensitivity analysis~\cite{brandes2007stabilityanalysis,buskens2006parametricsensitivity}, large-scale optimization~\cite{akcelik2006parallelpdecon,biegler2007pdeconstrainedopt,zahr2015progressive}, and uncertainty quantification~\cite{oden2004verification,biehler2014efficientuq,na2012reliability,smith2013uqbook}.
This work focuses on hyper-differential sensitivity analysis~(HDSA) \cite{hart2023hdsaillposed,hart2023hdsaoptimal,hart2025hdsaposterior,hart2020hdsapde,hart2025hdsaprior,stevens2022hdsaodes,sunseri2024hdsabayesian,sunseri2020hdsainverse}, a recent approach to deriving sensitivities for large-scale optimization problems with partial differential equations (PDEs) as constraints.
In particular, we focus on HDSA in the context of model discrepancy (HDSA-MD), meaning the constraint equations used in the optimization are deficient or only approximate \cite{hart2023hdsaoptimal,hart2025hdsaposterior}.
This is useful for cases in which (1) numerical models that fully and accurately reflect the true PDE dynamics are computationally expensive to solve --- so that performing adjoint-based optimization with such models is infeasible --- but a computationally inexpensive reduced-physics, low-fidelity, or reduced-order numerical model for the governing PDEs is available, or (2) the true system dynamics are unknown, but limited experimental data and an approximate numerical model are available.
The main idea is to solve the optimization problem with the inexpensive-but-deficient model, derive sensitivities of the solution with respect to the model form error, and use those sensitivities to update the solution to better reflect the true dynamics.
This procedure requires many forward and backward/adjoint solves of the inexpensive model, as well as access to a small number of forward solves from the expensive-but-accurate model.

\section{Hyper-differential sensitivity analysis with respect to model discrepancy}
\label{sec:hdsa}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/hdsa-diagram.pdf}
    \caption{The HDSA-MD framework. The discrepancy between the true optimization problem and an inexpensive approximate optimization problem is calibrated with available data, leading to an update formula from the solution of the approximate problem to the solution of the true optimization problem.
    }
    \label{fig:diagram}
\end{figure}

This section gives a mathematical description of HDSA-MD, which leverages post-optimality sensitivities in the presence of model form insufficiencies to solve PDE-constrained optimization problems with expensive or inaccessible constraint models.
The main idea of HDSA-MD is to first solve an optimization problem where the constraints are given by a computationally efficient approximate model, then update the problem solution based on limited data samples from the original large-scale optimization constraints.
The discrepancy between the true and approximate models is parameterized, calibrated, and used to transfer uncertainties in the input spaces to the updated solution.
\Cref{sec:optimization} establishes the setting of constrained optimization and adjoint-based solvers.
The problem is modified to account for discrepancies in the model form in \Cref{sec:model-discrepancy}, and \Cref{sec:hdsa-algorithm} outlines the strategy for using limited data to improve the quality of the solution despite model inaccuracies.
The overall procedure is summarized in \Cref{fig:diagram}.

\subsection{PDE-constrained optimization}
\label{sec:optimization}

Consider the following constrained minimization problem:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:optimization-constrained}
    \begin{aligned}
    \min_{\u,\z} ~& J(\u,\z)
    \qquad
    \text{where}~~& \c(\u,\z) = \0.
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
Here, $\u\in\R^{n_u}$ is the state variable, $\z\in\R^{n_z}$ is the control variable, $J:\R^{n_u}\times\R^{n_z}\to\R$ is the objective function, and $\c:\R^{n_u}\times\R^{n_z}\to\R^{n_u}$ specifies the constraints.
% Here, $n_u\in\mathbb{N}$ is the state dimension and $n_z\in\mathbb{N}$ is the control dimension.
In the context of PDE-constrained optimization, $\c$ represents the residual of a fully discretized PDE, for example after applying a finite element discretization and, if the problem is time-dependent, a time integration scheme.
If $\c(\cdot,\z)=\0$ admits a unique state solution over an admissible set of controls $Z \subset\R^{n_z}$ --- that is, if the discretized PDE is well posed for $\z\in Z$ --- then there exists a solution operator $\s:Z\to U \coloneqq \s(Z) \subset \R^{n_u}$ such that $\c(\s(\z),\z)=\0$ for all $\z\in Z$.
In this case, \cref{eq:optimization-constrained} can be formulated as the equivalent optimization problem
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:optimization-reducedspace}
    \min_{\z\in Z} \hat{J}(\z)
    \coloneqq J(\s(\z),\z).
\end{align}
The problem~\cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} is often called the \emph{reduced-space formulation} of \cref{eq:optimization-constrained}.

Local minima of \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} can be computed with standard optimization strategies as long as the derivatives of $\hat{J}$ can be computed efficiently.
The adjoint method~\cite{courant1962physics,givoli2021adjoint,tarantola2005inverse} provides expressions for the derivatives of $\hat{J}$ in terms of the (partial) derivatives of $J$ and $\c$.
Applying the chain rule,
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:gradJ-chain-rule}
    \grad{z}\hat{J}(\z)
    = \nabla[J(\s(\z),\z)]
    = \s_z(\z)\trp\grad{u}J(\s(\z),\z) + \grad{z}J(\s(\z),\z),
\end{align}
where $\s_z(\z)\in\R^{n_u\times n_z}$ is the $\z$ Jacobian of $\s$.
Note that we are using the convention that the gradient is a column vector.\footnote{If the gradient is defined as a row vector, the mathematics are equivalent but read slightly differently. For example,~\cref{eq:gradJ-chain-rule} would be $\grad{z}\hat{J}(\z) = \grad{u}J(\s(\z),\z)\s_z(\z) + \grad{z}J(\s(\z),\z)$. The choice of convention does not affect the implementation.}
Since $\c(\s(\z),\z) = \0$, and assuming that $\c$ and $\s$ are sufficiently smooth, the implicit function theorem yields the formula
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:Sz-implicit-function}
    \s_z(\z)
    = -\c_u(\s(\z),\z)^{-1}\c_z(\s(\z),\z),
\end{align}
where $\c_u(\u,\z)\in\mathbb{R}^{n_u\times n_u}$ and $\c_z(\u,\z)\in\mathbb{R}^{n_u\times n_z}$ denote the $\u$ and $\z$ Jacobians of $\c$, respectively.
Writing $\u = \s(\z)$, the first term in \cref{eq:gradJ-chain-rule} becomes
\begin{align}
    \begin{aligned}
    \s_z(\z)\trp\grad{u}J(\s(\z),\z)
    &= \left(-\c_u(\u,\z)^{-1}\c_z(\u,\z)\right)\trp\grad{u}J(\u,\z)
    \\
    &= -\c_z(\u,\z)\trp\c_u(\u,\z)\invtrp\grad{u}J(\u,\z)
    = \c_z(\u,\z)\trp\bflambda,
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
where $\bflambda \coloneqq -\c_u(\u,\z)\invtrp\grad{u}J(\u,\z) \in \R^{n_u}$ is the \emph{adjoint state} and resides in the dual of the state space.
Hence, for a given $\z$, the gradient $\grad{z}\hat{J}(\z)$ in \cref{eq:gradJ-chain-rule} can be computed by assembling or applying the derivatives of $J$ and $\c$, as summarized in \Cref{alg:adjoint-gradient}.
Similar arguments lead to an expression for the product of the Hessian $\grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z) \in\R^{n_z\times n_z}$ with a vector $\v\in\R^{n_z}$ in terms of the second-order derivatives of $J$ and $\c$, detailed in \Cref{alg:adjoint-hessvec}.
With these derivatives on hand, \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} can be solved with a Newton--Raphson approach, i.e., a variation on the iteration
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:newton-cg}
    \z_{k+1}
    = \z_{k} - \gamma_k\boldsymbol{\eta}_k,
    \qquad
    \grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z_{k})\boldsymbol{\eta}_{k} = \grad{z}\hat{J}(\z_{k}),
\end{align}
where $\gamma_k > 0$ is the step size, chosen to satisfy standard Wolfe or Armijo conditions~\cite{armijo1966minimization,wolfe1969conditions}.
The linear system for the step direction $\boldsymbol{\eta}_{k}$ may be solved, for example, via the conjugate gradient method~\cite{hestenes1952conjugategradient}, which only requires Hessian-vector applications $\v\mapsto\grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z_k)\v$ instead of forming $\grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z_k)$ explicitly.
Note that the computational cost of this approach is typically dominated by the cost of evaluating the solver $\s(\z)$ and solving adjoint equations involving the Jacobians of $\c$.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{AdjointGradient}{~$\z\in\R^{n_z}$~}
    % \State $\u \gets \s(\z)\in\R^{n_u}$
    \State Solve $\c(\u, \z) = \0$ for $\u$, i.e., $\u \gets \s(\z)$
        \Comment{Solve the (PDE) constraint.}
    \State Solve $\c_u(\u,\z)\trp \bflambda = - \grad{u}J(\u,\z)$ for $\bflambda$
        \Comment{Compute the adjoint.}
    \State \textbf{return} $\c_z(\u,\z)\trp \bflambda + \grad{z}J(\u,\z)$
        \Comment{Assemble the gradient.}
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\caption{Adjoint-based gradient $\grad{z}\hat{J}(\z)$ for minimizing \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace}.}
\label{alg:adjoint-gradient}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{AdjointHessvec}{
    % State
    $\u\in\R^{n_u}$,
    % control
    $\z\in\R^{n_z}$,
    % adjoint
    $\bflambda\in\R^{n_u}$,
    % search direction
    $\v\in\R^{n_z}$
}
    \State Solve $\c_u(\u,\z) \bfmu = -\c_z(\u,\z) \v$ for $\bfmu$
        % \Comment{Compute the incremental adjoint.}
    \State $\x_J \gets \grad{z,u}J(\u,\z) \bfmu +  \grad{z,z}J(\u,\z) \v$
    \State $\y_J \gets \grad{u,u}J(\u,\z) \bfmu + \grad{u,z}J(\u,\z) \v$
    \State $\y_c \gets \bflambda\trp \c_{u,u}(\u,\z) \bfmu +  \bflambda\trp \c_{u,z}(\u,\z) \v$
    \State Solve $\c_u(\u,\z)\trp \bfgamma = -(\y_J + \y_c)$ for $\bfgamma$
    \State $\x_c \gets \c_z(\u,\z)\trp \bfgamma + \bflambda\trp \c_{z,u}(\u,\z) \bfmu + \bflambda\trp \c_{z,z}(\u,\z)\v$
    \State \textbf{return} $\x_J + \x_c$
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\caption{Adjoint-based Hessian-vector product $\v\mapsto\grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z)\v$ for minimizing \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace}.}
\label{alg:adjoint-hessvec}
\end{algorithm}

A software implementation for solving \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} through \cref{eq:newton-cg} with \Cref{alg:adjoint-gradient,alg:adjoint-hessvec} requires several ingredients, namely the constraint solver $\s:\z\mapsto\u$ and the derivatives of $J$ and $\c$.
The software libraries introduced in later sections define abstract templates with methods for each ingredient, to be implemented by the user or handled with automatic differentiation when possible.

\subsection{Model discrepancy sensitivities}
\label{sec:model-discrepancy}

There are several scenarios in which solving \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} is computationally infeasible: (1) solving the constraint and/or forming its derivatives is possible, but highly computationally expensive; (2) the constraint is available only as a black box, meaning it can be evaluated but not differentiated; and (3) the constraint is unknown or unavailable for computation.
In each scenario, one remedy is to replace the constraint with a computationally tractable model based on simplified physics, model order reduction, or data-driven surrogate modeling.
To that end, let $\tilde{\c}:\R^{n_u}\times\R^{n_z}\to\R^{n_u}$ be an approximation to the original constraint $\c$ with corresponding solution map $\tilde{\s}:Z\to U$, i.e., $\tilde{\c}(\tilde{\s}(\z), \z) = \0$ for all $\z\in Z$, and consider the reduced-space problem
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:optimization-approx}
    \min_{\z\in Z}
    \tilde{J}(\z) \coloneqq
    J(\tilde{\s}(\z), \z).
\end{align}
The discrepancy between the model constraint $\tilde{\c}$ and the true constraint $\c$ may cause minimizers of \cref{eq:optimization-approx} to be far from minimizers of \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace}.
We will show, however, that solutions of \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} can be written in terms of solutions of \cref{eq:optimization-approx}.
% in which $\tilde{\c}(\tilde{\s}(\z), \z) = \0$ for all $\z\in Z$.
If $\tilde{\s}$ is less expensive to evaluate than $\s$, and if the derivatives of $\tilde{\c}$ are more easily formed than the derivatives of $\c$, then solving \cref{eq:optimization-approx} and strategically updating that solution is a computationally tractable alternative to solving \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} directly.

To relate \cref{eq:optimization-approx} to \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace}, let $\bfdelta \coloneqq \bfdelta(\z;\bftheta)\in\R^{n_z}$ for $\bftheta\in\R^{n_\theta}$ be a smooth function, parameterized such that $\bfdelta(\z;\0) = \0$ for all $\z$ (hence $\bfdelta_z(\z;\0)$ is the zero matrix), and consider the perturbed objective function
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:optimization-discrepancy}
    \J(\z, \bftheta)
    \coloneqq J\big(\tilde{\s}(\z) + \bfdelta(\z;\bftheta), \z\big).
\end{align}
We call $\bfdelta(\z;\bftheta)$ the \emph{discrepancy term}.
By construction,
$
    \J(\z,\0)
    % = J(\tilde{\s}(\z) + \bfdelta(\z;\0),\z)
    = J(\tilde{\s}(\z),\z)
    = \tilde{J}(\z),
$
the objective function in the approximate problem~\cref{eq:optimization-approx}.
On the other hand, if $\bftheta$ is such that $\tilde{\s}(\z) + \bfdelta(\z;\bftheta) \approx \s(\z)$ for all $\z$, then
$
    \J(\z, \bftheta)
    % = J(\tilde{\s}(\z) + \bfdelta(\z;\bftheta), \z)
    \approx
    J(\s(\z), \z)
    = \hat{J}(\z),
$
the objective function for the original optimization problem \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace}.

Next, let $\tilde{\z}\in\R^{n_z}$ be a local minimizer of~\cref{eq:optimization-approx}.
Fixing $\bftheta = \0$, the necessary optimality conditions give
\begin{align*}
    \grad{z}\J(\z,\0)
    = \grad{z}\tilde{J}(\z)
    = \0
\end{align*}
and that $\grad{z,z}\J(\z,\0)$ is positive definite.
By the implicit function theorem, there exists a smooth function $\f:\R^{n_\theta}\to\R^{n_z}$ such that $\f(\0) = \tilde{\z}$ and $\grad{z}\J(\f(\bftheta), \bftheta) = \0$ for all $\bftheta$ in a neighborhood of $\bftheta = \0$, where $\f$ has the $n_z \times n_\theta$ Jacobian
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:theta-jacobian}
    \f_\theta(\bftheta)
    = - \grad{z,z}\J(\f(\bftheta), \bftheta)^{-1}\grad{z,\theta}\J(\f(\bftheta), \bftheta).
    % = -\H(\bftheta)^{-1}\B(\bftheta),
\end{align}
Now let $\z'$ be a solution to the original problem \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace}.
If we can find $\bftheta'$ such that $\z' = \f(\bftheta')$, then linearizing $\f$ around $\bftheta = \0$ yields the approximation
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:control-update}
    \z'
    = \f(\bftheta')
    \approx \f(\0) + \f_\theta(\0)\bftheta'
    = \tilde{\z} + \f_\theta(\0)\bftheta',
\end{align}
which is an update formula from the solution $\tilde{\z}$ of \cref{eq:optimization-approx} to the solution $\z'$ of \cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace}.

\subsection{Hyper-differential sensitivity analysis}
\label{sec:hdsa-algorithm}

Computing $\z'$ with \cref{eq:control-update} requires three components:
the approximate problem solution $\tilde{\z}$, which can computed by applying the adjoint method to \cref{eq:optimization-approx};
the Jacobian $\f_\theta$ given in \cref{eq:theta-jacobian} at $\bftheta=\0$;
and the optimal discrepancy parameters $\bftheta'$, to be chosen so that $\tilde{\s}(\z) + \bfdelta(\z;\bftheta') \approx \s(\z)$.
\Cref{alg:adjoint-gradient,alg:adjoint-hessvec} describe the ingredients needed to compute $\tilde{\z}$ (replacing $\c$ with $\tilde{\c}$, $\s$ with $\tilde{\s}$, and $\hat{J}$ with $\tilde{J}$).
For $\f_\theta(\0)$, let $\H \coloneqq \grad{z,z}\J(\f(\0),\0) \in \R^{n_z\times n_z}$ and note that, since $\bftheta=\0$, $\H = \grad{z,z}\tilde{J}(\tilde{\z})$, which is calculated in \Cref{alg:adjoint-hessvec}.
The remaining piece is
$\B \coloneqq \grad{z,\theta}\J(\f(\0),\0)\in\R^{n_z\times n_\theta}$ so that $\f_\theta(\0) = -\H^{-1}\B$.
Observe, with some abbreviation, that
\begin{align}
    \begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:B-expanded}
    \grad{z,\theta}\J(\z, \bftheta)
    % &= \grad{\theta}\grad{z}[J(\tilde{\s}(\z) + \bfdelta(\z;\bftheta),\z)]
    &= \grad{\theta}\grad{z}[J(\tilde{\s} + \bfdelta,\z)]
    % \\
    % &= \grad{\theta}[
    %     (\tilde{\s}_z(\z) + \bfdelta_z(\z;\bftheta))\trp\grad{u}J(\tilde{\s}(\z) + \bfdelta(\z;\bftheta),\z)
    %     + \grad{z}J(\tilde{s}(\z)+\bfdelta(\z;\bftheta), \z)
    % ]
    \\
    &= \grad{\theta}[
        (\tilde{\s}_z + \bfdelta_z)\trp\grad{u}J(\tilde{\s} + \bfdelta,\z)
        + \grad{z}J(\tilde{\s} + \bfdelta, \z)
    ]
    \\
    &= (\tilde{\s}_{z} + \bfdelta_{z})\trp\grad{u,u}J(\tilde{\s} + \bfdelta, \z)\bfdelta_\theta
    + \grad{u}J(\tilde{\s} + \bfdelta, \z)\trp\bfdelta_{z,\theta}
    + \grad{z,u}J(\tilde{\s} + \bfdelta, \z)\bfdelta_\theta.
    \end{aligned}
\end{align}
Evaluated at $(\z,\bftheta) = (\f(\0),\0) = (\tilde{\z},\0)$ and recalling $\bfdelta_z(\z,\0) = \0$, this simplifies to give
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:B-matrix}
    \B
    % = \tilde{\s}_z(\tilde{\z})\trp\grad{u,u}J(\tilde{\s}(\tilde{\z}),\tilde{\z})\bfdelta_\theta(\0)
    % + \grad{u}J(\tilde{\s}(\tilde{\z}),\tilde{\z})\trp\bfdelta_{z,\theta}(\0)
    % + \nabla_{z,u}J(\tilde{\s}(\tilde{\z}), \tilde{\z})\bfdelta_\theta(\0).
    = \tilde{\s}_z(\tilde{\z})\trp\grad{u,u}J(\tilde{\u},\tilde{\z})\bfdelta_\theta(\tilde{\z},\0)
    + \grad{u}J(\tilde{\u},\tilde{\z})\trp\bfdelta_{z,\theta}(\tilde{\z},\0)
    + \nabla_{z,u}J(\tilde{\u}, \tilde{\z})\bfdelta_\theta(\tilde{\z},\0),
\end{align}
where $\tilde{\u} \coloneqq \tilde{\s}(\tilde{\z})$.
%
Because $\H$ is independent of the discrepancy term $\bfdelta$, and since $\B$ depends only on $\bfdelta_\theta(\tilde{\z},\0)$ and $\bfdelta_{z,\theta}(\tilde{\z},\0)$, an application of the Riesz representation theorem leads to a linear-affine form for $\bfdelta$,
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:affine-discrepancy}
    \bfdelta(\z,\bftheta) = \left[\begin{array}{cc}
        \I_{n_u} & \I_{n_u}\otimes\z\trp\M_z
    \end{array}\right]\bftheta,
\end{align}
where $\otimes$ denotes the Kronecker product~\cite{vanloan2000kronecker} and $\M_z\in\R^{n_z\times n_z}$ is a mass matrix defining an appropriate inner product $\langle\z_1,\z_2\rangle = \sqrt{\z_1\trp\M_z\z_2}$ on $Z\subset\R^{n_z}$, the space of admissible controls (see \cite{hart2023hdsaoptimal} for more detail).
Hence, the dimension of $\bftheta$ is $n_\theta = n_u(n_z + 1)$.
The Kronecker structure of \cref{eq:affine-discrepancy} enables efficient matrix-vector products $\v\mapsto\B\v$ without assembling $\B$ explicitly, which we will discuss shortly.

The final item needed for \cref{eq:control-update} is an optimal $\bftheta'$ such that $\bfdelta(\z;\bftheta') \approx \s(\z) - \tilde{\s}(\z)$.
Although solving~\cref{eq:optimization-reducedspace} directly is intractable due to the derivatives of the full constraint $\c$ being inaccessible or highly expensive, we assume that we can either (1) solve the constraint equation (i.e., evaluate $\s(\z)$) a limited number of times or (2) in a data-driven scenario, observe a limited number of state solutions with known controls.
To that end, for a linearly independent set of controls $\{\z_{\ell}\}_{\ell=1}^{n_d}$ for which we have access to $\s(\z_1),\ldots,\s(\z_{n_d})$ and $\tilde{\s}(\z_1),\ldots,\tilde{\s}(\z_{n_d})$, we consider the Bayesian linear inference problem $\A\bftheta = \mathbf{d} + \bfeta$, where
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:bayesian-inference}
    \A =
    \left[\begin{array}{cc}
        \I_{n_u} & \I_{n_u}\otimes\z_1\trp\M_z\\
        \vdots & \vdots \\
        \I_{n_u} & \I_{n_u}\otimes\z_{n_d}\trp\M_z
    \end{array}\right],
    % \in\mathbb{R}^{n_u n_d \times n_\theta},
    \quad
    \mathbf{d}
    = \left[\begin{array}{c}
        \s(\z_1) - \tilde{\s}(\z_1)
        \\ \vdots \\
        \s(\z_{n_d}) - \tilde{\s}(\z_{n_d})
    \end{array}\right],
    % \in\mathbb{R}^{n_u n_d},
    \quad
    \bfeta
    = \left[\begin{array}{c}
        \bfepsilon_1
        \\ \vdots \\
        \bfepsilon_{n_d}
    \end{array}\right],
    % \in\mathbb{R}^{n_u n_d},
\end{align}
and in which each $\bfepsilon_{\ell}\sim\mathcal{N}(\0, \alpha\M_u^{-1})$ is an uncorrelated Gaussian noise term with variance scaling $\alpha > 0$ and where $\M_u\in\R^{n_u\times n_u}$ is a mass matrix for the state space.
% Note that the $n_u n_d \times n_u(1 + n_z)$ data matrix $\A$ is large and that the system is likely underdetermined.
To develop a prior distribution for $\bftheta$, we impose priors for the state and control spaces.
Let $\W_u\in\R^{n_u\times n_u}$ and $\W_z\in\R^{n_z\times n_z}$ be precision (inverse covariance) matrices for the state and control spaces, respectively.
In the context of PDEs, these precision matrices are often {\color{royalblue}(inverse?)} elliptic operators.
It can be shown that an appropriate precision matrix for a zero-mean prior distribution on the parameter vector $\bftheta$ is
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:prior-parameter-precision}
    \W_\theta
    = \left[\begin{array}{cc}
        \W_u & \W_u \otimes \tilde{\z}\trp\M_z \\
        \W_u\otimes\M_z\tilde{\z} & \W_u\otimes\M_z(\W_z + \tilde{\z}\tilde{\z}\trp\M_z)
    \end{array}\right]
    \in\R^{n_\theta \times n_\theta}.
\end{align}
The posterior distribution for $\bftheta$ is also Gaussian, with the following mean and covariance:
\begin{subequations}
\label{eq:posterior-parameter-distribution}
\begin{align}
    \bar{\bftheta}
    &= \tfrac{1}{\alpha}\bfSigma\A\trp(\I_{n_d}\otimes\M_{u})\mathbf{d},
    \\
    \bfSigma_\theta
    &= \left(\W_{\theta} + \tfrac{1}{\alpha}\A\trp(\I_{n_d}\otimes \M_u)\A\right)^{-1}.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

Making appropriate choices for the prior distributions, sampling from the posterior distribution \cref{eq:posterior-parameter-distribution}, and propagating the result through the product $\bftheta\mapsto\H^{-1}\B\bftheta$ for \cref{eq:control-update} is nontrivial and is the subject of several previous works~\cite{hart2023hdsaoptimal,hart2025hdsaposterior,hart2025hdsaprior}.
One key result from these works is that computations in the high-dimensional state, control, or parameter spaces can be mitigated by taking advantage of the structure of $\B$ in \cref{eq:B-matrix}.
To make this precise, define
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:intermediate-quantitites}
\begin{align}
    \G &= \1\1\trp + (\Z - \tilde{\z}\1\trp)\trp\W_z^{-1}(\Z - \tilde{\z}\1\trp)\in\R^{n_d \times n_d},
    \\
    \Z &= [~\z_1~~\cdots~~\z_{n_d}~]\in\R^{n_z\times n_d},
\end{align}
where $\1\in\R^{n_d}$ is a vector of ones.
Let $(\mu_i,\g_i)$ be the $n_d$ eigenpairs of $\G$, and define
\begin{align}
    \y_i
    &= \Z\g_i - (\1\trp\g_i)\tilde{\z}
    \in\R^{n_z},
    % \\
    % s_i
    % &= (\1\trp\g_i) - \y_i\trp\W_{z}^{-1}\tilde{\z}
    % \in\R,
    \\
    \alpha_{\ell}
    &= 1 - \tilde{\z}\trp\W_{z}^{-1}(\z_\ell - \tilde{\z})
    \in\R,
    \\
    b_{i,\ell}
    &= (\z_\ell - \tilde{\z})\trp\W_{z}^{-1}\Z\g_i + (\1\trp\g_i)\alpha_\ell
    \in \R,
    \\
    \u_{\ell}
    &= \W_u^{-1}\M_u(\s(\z_\ell) - \tilde{\s}(\z_\ell))
    \in\R^{n_u},
    \\
    \u_{\ell,i}
    &= (\alpha \W_u + \mu_i\M_u)^{-1}\M_u \u_\ell
    \in\R^{n_u}
\end{align}
for $i,\ell = 1,\ldots, n_d$.
\end{subequations}

\section{\SABL: Prototyping HDSA-MD in \MATLAB}
\label{sec:sabl}

% \begin{purpose}
% Why \SABL exists. Instead of throwing newcomers headlong into \cpp, it's better to start in a high-level language.
% \end{purpose}

Implementing HDSA-MD poses two major challenges.
First, the mathematics are sufficiently complicated that a successful implementation necessitates careful verification at each step of the algorithm.
Second, the kinds of problems for which HDSA-MD is most useful are large scale, likely requiring HPC resources and distributed computing.
These are competing and conflicting interests that influence our choice of software language: on the one hand, a high-level language --- designed for high readability and ease of use --- would enable an intuitive translation of mathematics to code and simplify the process of checking intermediate steps, but may ultimately struggle to perform at scale; on the other hand, a low-level language --- optimized for performance but not for readability or implementation ease --- would require substantial expertise and be difficult to verify, but a successful implementation would ultimately be able to tackle large-scale problems.
To address the first issue, this section presents \SABL: \textbf{S}andbox for \textbf{A}djoint-\textbf{B}ased outer \textbf{L}oop analysis, a \MATLAB library that implements PDE-constrained optimization and HDSA-MD.
The source code is publicly available at \textbf{TODO}.
\SM{A version of \SABL and \HdsaLib should be made public for this paper (\texttt{GitHub/SandiaLabs/<SABL,HdsaLib>}?), which requires making a software release request.)}
Our purpose in this discussion is not to provide detailed package documentation, but rather to shed light on the design and implementation process and the associated challenges.
Writing a high-level library allows us to make design decisions with relatively little technical debt and enables rapid verification of intermediate parts of the procedure.
Once designed and tested, \SABL can be used a guide for developing a low-level HPC implementation of HDSA-MD in \cpp, which is the topic of \Cref{sec:hpc}.

\begin{remark}[Why \MATLAB?]
Other popular high-level languages such as \texttt{Python} and \texttt{Julia} could also be suitable for a prototype library, but \MATLAB has a few features that make it particularly attractive for our purposes.
It has a low barrier to entry, intuitive syntax, and is designed around linear algebra operations, which form the majority of the mathematics in HDSA-MD.
% At the same time, \MATLAB is interpreted (not compiled) and does not require imports or type declarations, all of which generally lower implementation cost compared to compiled languages.
Furthermore, \MATLAB features an object-oriented paradigm that closely mirrors \cpp class syntax, including \texttt{private} / \texttt{public} blocks and abstract methods (virtual functions).
Unlike open-source languages, \MATLAB also has strong backwards compatibility guarantees, leading to greater code longevity and no recurring maintenance needs.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Verification strategies}

Because the \MATLAB language is designed to resemble linear algebra, implementing many of the operations needed to compute the updated control $\z'$ is somewhat straightforward.
However, verifying the implementation and identifying coding errors can still pose a significant challenge due to the mathematical complexity of the framework, especially because many of the intermediate quantities (such as those in \cref{eq:intermediate-quantitites}) are not physically interpretable.
On the other hand, a few intermediate parts of the procedure can be checked directly.
We discuss two short examples here. \shanenote{other examples?}
These fine-grained ``sanity checks'' are easy to perform with a high-level language but can be surprisingly nontrivial in an HPC context.

First, the implementation of the derivatives of $J$ and $\c$ (or $\tilde{J}$ and $\tilde{\c}$) can be compared to finite difference estimates.
For example,
\begin{align}
    \frac{1}{\delta}(J(\u + \delta \e_i, \z) - J(\u))
    \approx \e_i\trp \grad{u}J(\u, \z) = (\grad{u}J(\u,\z))_{i},
\end{align}
where $\e_i$ is the $i$-th canonical basis vector.
The left-hand side can can be evaluated for various small $\delta$ using the method for $J(\u,\z)$ and compared against the method for computing $\grad{u}J(\u,\z)$.
This check is included in \Cref{code:sabl-optimization} and made available to the user because the derivatives of $J$ and $\c$ are implemented by the user.
Second, we expect every Hessian matrix to be symmetric positive definite.
\Cref{alg:adjoint-hessvec} and \Cref{table:hdsa-requirements} are written with Hessian-vector products only, but if the problem dimensions are not large, the Hessian matrix can be constructed explicitly and examined.
For example, \Cref{alg:adjoint-hessvec} can be partially verified by constructing
\begin{align}
    \grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z)
    = \left[\begin{array}{c|c|c}
        \grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z)\e_1
        & \cdots &
        \grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z)\e_{n_z}
    \end{array}\right]
\end{align}
and performing an eigendecomposition to check that all eigenvalues are real and positive.

In addition to fine-grained (unit) tests, it is important to probe the correctness and performance of the overall framework with end-to-end (regression) tests.
However, as with many outer-loop procedures, there is no obvious strategy for formally verifying HDSA-MD as a whole.
This is in contrast to numerical PDE solvers, for which end-to-end tests are often conducted via the method of manufactured solutions~(MMS)~\cite{roache2001manufacturedsolutions}.
For example, a solver for the problem $\mathcal{L}(u) = f$ can be verified by first choosing an analytical function $u$, applying the differential operator $\mathcal{L}$ by hand to determine $f$, then using the numerical solver with that choice of $f$ (for varying mesh sizes, etc.) to recover $u$.
% Similar strategies exist for inverse problems: select the solution first, determine observations, apply an algorithm to the observations, and compare the results with the truth.
This approach does not readily extend to HDSA-MD, which consists not only of forward PDE solves (applying $\s$ and $\tilde{\s}$), but also optimization problems (computing $\tilde{\z}$) and a sensitivity analysis (updating $\tilde{\z} \to \z'$) involving the specification and interaction of several probability distributions.
Our best alternative for end-to-end verification is to construct a problem~\cref{eq:optimization-constrained} that is simple and low-dimensional enough to be solved directly with \Cref{alg:adjoint-gradient,alg:adjoint-hessvec}, resulting in a true control solution $\z^{*}$.
An artificial approximate problem \cref{eq:optimization-approx} can then be constructed, and HDSA-MD applied, to produce an updated control $\z'$ that can be compared to $\z^{*}$.
We provide one such simple example now.

\subsection{A simple test problem}
\label{sec:smallexample}

Consider the minimization problem
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:advection-diffusion-continuous-objective}
    \min_{z} \frac{1}{2}\int_{0}^{1}\big(
        s(x;z) - \phi(x)
    \big)^2\,dx
    + \frac{\beta}{2}\int_{0}^{1}z(x)^2\,dx,
\end{align}
where $s(x;z)$ solves the one-dimensional advection-diffusion equation with Robin boundary conditions,
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:advection-diffusion}
\begin{gather}
\label{eq:advection-diffusion-dynamics}
-\kappa u''(x) + v u'(x) = z(x),
\quad x\in(0, 1),
\\
\label{eq:advection-diffusion-boundaries}
\kappa u'(x=0) = h u(x=0),
\qquad
-\kappa u'(x=1) = h u(x=1),
\end{gather}
\end{subequations}
in which $u,z,\phi:[0,1]\to\R$ and $\kappa, v, h$ are constants.
The goal is to select an appropriate forcing function $z$ such that the state $u(x) = s(x;z)$ matches the given target function.
This problem was used previously in~\cite{hart2025hdsaposterior}; different from that work, the subject of the discussion here is the verification process.

A first-order uniform finite element discretization of $[0,1]$ with standard linear finite elements and $n_u$ degrees of freedom approximates \cref{eq:advection-diffusion} with the linear system
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:advection-diffusion-discrete}
    (\kappa\S + v\V + h\B)\u = \M\z,
\end{align}
where $\u\in\R^{n_u}$ is the discretized state, $\z\in\R^{n_z}$ is the discretized control with $n_z = n_u$, and the matrices $\S,\V,\B,\M\in\R^{n_u\times n_u}$ model the diffusion, advection, boundary conditions, and the spatial inner product, respectively.
The discrete constraint function is therefore given by $\c(\u,\z) = \A\u - \M\z$, with associated solution operator $\s(\z) = \A^{-1}\M\z$, where $\A = \kappa\S + v\V + h\B$.
Applying the spatial discretization to~\cref{eq:advection-diffusion-continuous-objective} results in the objective function
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:advection-diffusion-discrete-objective}
    J(\u, \z)
    = \frac{1}{2}(\u - \bfphi)\trp\M(\u - \bfphi)
    + \frac{\beta}{2}\z\trp\M\z,
\end{align}
in which $\bfphi\in\R^{n_u}$ discretizes the target function $\phi(x)$.
For moderate $n_u$, \cref{eq:advection-diffusion-discrete}--\cref{eq:advection-diffusion-discrete-objective} constitutes a tractable PDE-constrained optimization problem of the form \cref{eq:optimization-constrained}.
We set $\beta = 10^{-3}$ in this experiment.

For verification, we begin by checking the discretized dynamics \cref{eq:advection-diffusion-discrete} through MMS.
We then implement the derivatives of $J$ and $\c$, comparing them to finite differences and testing for symmetry or positive definiteness when appropriate.

The implementation of the adjoint method derivatives $\grad{z}\hat{J}(\z)$ and $\grad{z,z}\hat{J}(\z)\v$ from \Cref{alg:adjoint-gradient,alg:adjoint-hessvec} can then be checked through finite differences of the reduced-space objective $\hat{J}(\z) = J(\s(\z),\z)$.
Next, an MMS-type approach can verify the optimization procedure by (1) choosing an analytical $u^{*}$ satisfying the boundary conditions \cref{eq:advection-diffusion-boundaries}, (2) determining a corresponding $z^{*}$ from the dynamics \cref{eq:advection-diffusion-discrete}, (3) defining $\phi = u^{*}$ and setting $\beta$ small (so that the objective function can attain a minimal value near zero), and (4) solving the optimization problem, which should yield $z^{*}$ as the minimizer.

With the numerical PDE solver and optimization method verified, we proceed by considering a simplified physics model as a substitute for \cref{eq:advection-diffusion}:
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:just-diffusion}
\begin{gather}
\label{eq:just-diffusion-dynamics}
-\kappa u''(x) = z(x),
\quad x\in(0, 1),
\\
\label{eq:just-diffusion-boundaries}
\kappa u'(x=0) = h u(x=0),
\qquad
-\kappa u'(x=1) = h u(x=1).
\end{gather}
\end{subequations}
This diffusion equation is \cref{eq:advection-diffusion} with $v = 0$, i.e., no advection, which may be a physically relevant approximation for \cref{eq:advection-diffusion} when the advection constant $v$ is unknown.
The discretized dynamics are given by
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:diffusion-discrete}
    (\kappa\S + h\B)\u = \M\z,
\end{align}
which gives rise to the approximate constraint $\tilde{\c}(\u,\z) = \A_\textrm{d}\u - \M\z$, with solution operator $\tilde{\s}(\z) = \A_\textrm{d}^{-1}\M\z$, where $\A_\text{d} = \kappa\S + h\B$.
An MMS-type check can again be applied to verify \cref{eq:diffusion-discrete} and the optimization of $\tilde{J}(\z) = J(\tilde{\s}(\z),\z)$ to produce $\tilde{\z}$.
% For these experiments, we use $n_u = 200$ grid points.

In this simple test problem, we can minimize $J(\s(\z),\z)$ directly to obtain the true optimizer $\z^{*}$, see \Cref{fig:simple-hdsa}.
The target function $\phi$ can be well approximated by both the original model~\cref{eq:advection-diffusion} and the approximate model~\cref{eq:just-diffusion}, that is, the objective functions $J(\s(\z),\z)$ and $J(\tilde{\s}(\z),\z)$ can both be driven to near zero; the issue is that using the approximate control $\tilde{\z}$ in the original model \cref{eq:advection-diffusion} results in a state $\s(\tilde{\z})$ which does not approximate the target well, i.e., $J(\s(\tilde{\z}), \tilde{\z})$ is large even though $J(\tilde{\s}(\tilde{\z}), \tilde{\z})$ is small.
Hence, \cref{eq:just-diffusion} is not a good surrogate for \cref{eq:advection-diffusion} for the purpose of optimization unless we account for the discrepancy between the two models.
Applying HDSA-MD with $n_d = 2$ data samples, using $\z_1 = \tilde{\z}$ and $\z_2$ a vector of all ones, produces an updated control $\z'$.

\section{\HdsaLib: High-performance HDSA-MD in \cpp}
\label{sec:hpc}

Although \MATLAB enables rapid prototyping and intuitive mathematical syntax, large scale HDSA-MD problems --- for example those arising from PDEs defined in three spatial dimensions so that the state dimension $n_u$ is very large --- necessitate an implementation that can interact with HPC tools.
This section introduces \HdsaLib, a \cpp library that implements HDSA-MD using the same design structure as \SABL.
Working in \cpp requires low-level memory management (pointers), custom data structures, and in general more boiler-plate code than is required in \MATLAB.
On the other hand, these features enable parallelization and state-of-the-art compute performance.
Whereas \SABL includes its own forward solver and optimization routines, \HdsaLib relies on interfaces to other high-performance \cpp codes for these tasks.

\subsection{Object-oriented design following \SABL}

\HdsaLib uses the same object-oriented abstract interface paradigm as \SABL.
Using the code architecture and conventions established in \SABL as a guide for \HdsaLib significantly eases the intellectual implementation burden and allows the developer to focus on the technical aspects of \cpp rather than the algorithmic approach to HDSA-MD.
For instance, \HdsaLib defines its own \texttt{Vector} data structure and implements randomized linear algebra routines for generalized SVD and eigenvalue problems, all of which are taken for granted in \MATLAB.
\Cref{code:hdsalib-hdsa} shows a \cpp class in \HdsaLib that follows the design of the \MATLAB class in \SABL included in \Cref{code:sabl-hdsa}.
The only substantive difference from the \MATLAB version is additional \cpp boilerplate code.

\subsection{\MrHyDE: A high-performance \cpp library for solving differential equations}
\label{sec:mrhyde}

Implementing HDSA-MD at scale requires several HPC capabilities, including efficient matrix-vector computations, linear and nonlinear system solvers, forward numerical PDE solvers for a variety of relevant dynamics, time steppers, differentiation of forward solvers and other functions, and, perhaps most challengingly, all of these tasks should be accomplished in parallel.
To meet these requirements, we leverage \MrHyDE, a software library for the solution of \textbf{M}ulti-\textbf{r}esolution \textbf{Hy}bridized \textbf{D}ifferential \textbf{E}quations, publicly available at \href{https://github.com/sandialabs/MrHyDE}{\texttt{github.com/sandialabs/MrHyDE}}.
\SM{@Tim best current way to cite \MrHyDE?}
\MrHyDE builds on builds on the advanced \cpp HPC libraries \texttt{trilinos}~\cite{trilinos2020} and \texttt{kokkos}~\cite{edwards2014kokkos,trott2022kokkos} to solve transient nonlinear strongly coupled multiphysics/multiscale problems and to enable outer-loop analyses through automatic differentiation.
The user specifies the weak form of the governing physics, a computational mesh and geometry, and any system parameters; corresponding finite elements are then assembled automatically in parallel.
Available physics include, but are not limited to, convection-diffusion-reaction systems, Helmholtz equations, linear elasticity, crystal elasticity, Stokes flow, Navier--Stokes equations, Maxwell's equations, single-phase incompressible flow in porous media, and nonlinear heat diffusion.
Post-processing options are available to compute quantities of interest, e.g., an objective function in the context of optimization.

To fully leverage \MrHyDE for HDSA-MD, \HdsaLib includes \MrHyDE-specific interfaces that convert between the data structures used in each library.

\subsection{Verification by comparison with \SABL}

The complexities of a full HPC environment often make it difficult to assess and verify implementations of novel, complicated algorithms such as HDSA-MD.
For example, it is not always convenient to do even simple unit-level tests such as testing matrices for positive definiteness.
This was one reason for developing \SABL, where many such checks are straightforward.
In addition, \SABL can be used to compare to \HdsaLib with \MrHyDE on simple problems.
For example, we can re-implement the example in \Cref{sec:smallexample} in \HdsaLib and compare the results from each library.
This is not an ideal verification strategy: comparing two software libraries can only show if two implementations are equivalent, not if one or both implementation is mathematically correct.
However, due to the complexities of HDSA-MD and the complications of working in a full HPC enviroment, this is our best option for verification.
After verifying results on simple problems that can also be solved in \SABL, we have more confidence to dive into a large-scale problem that cannot be solved with \SABL.
